Money You Owe Family Before Bankruptcy - The Insider Preference Rule

If you paid your mom back $4,000 last summer and you file in October, the trustee can sue her for the money. Here is how the rule works and how to plan around it.

The single most painful surprise for people considering bankruptcy is learning that debts owed to family are not "private." Under 11 U.S.C. § 547(b), payments to insiders in the year before filing are preferences — transfers the trustee can undo by suing the recipient. Your mom, your sister, your brother-in-law, your best friend from college: if you paid them back and then filed bankruptcy within the lookback window, they get sued.

This rule is not a trap set by individual trustees. It is the statutory core of how bankruptcy treats creditors equally. The policy: you cannot use the last few months before filing to shuttle money to favored creditors at the expense of everyone else. Family is a favored creditor. The rule treats them that way.

Who Counts as an Insider

Under 11 U.S.C. § 101(31)(A), "insider" when the debtor is an individual means:

  • Relatives — parents, siblings, children, grandchildren, in-laws, spouse. "Relative" under § 101(45) includes anyone related by affinity within the third degree.
  • Partnerships where the debtor is a general partner, and the partners themselves.
  • Corporations the debtor controls, and officers, directors, and persons in control of those corporations.
  • General partnerships the debtor is a member of.

The statutory list is non-exclusive. Courts have extended insider status to unmarried domestic partners (In re Schuman, 81 B.R. 583 (9th Cir. BAP 1988)), close friends with a history of financial entanglement, and business associates in a long-term relationship. The test courts apply is whether the person has a "close relationship" with the debtor such that the transaction was not at arm's length.

Practical read: assume anyone you would describe as "close family" or "a close friend I owe money to" is an insider. The doubtful cases — second cousins, old college friends, distant in-laws — are fact-specific, and trustees generally err on the side of including them in the preference action.

The Lookback Windows

90 days — non-insider creditors (§ 547(b)(4)(A))

Payments to regular creditors (credit cards, medical providers, car lenders, utility companies) in the 90 days before filing can be clawed back if they meet the preference elements below. Most routine monthly payments are protected by ordinary-course-of-business defenses, but one-time payoffs or lump-sum reductions are exposed.

1 year — insiders (§ 547(b)(4)(B))

The extended lookback applies specifically when the creditor is an insider. This is the rule that catches family-loan repayments. The clock runs from the date of transfer (when the funds actually moved), not from when the loan was made.

2 years — fraudulent transfers (§ 548)

Separate from preferences, § 548 lets the trustee undo any transfer made with actual intent to hinder creditors, or made for less than reasonably equivalent value while the debtor was insolvent. The federal lookback is 2 years. Unlike preferences, fraudulent-transfer claims do not require the recipient to be a creditor — gifts to family, sales at below-market, and transfers into trusts all fall in scope.

4 years or longer — state fraudulent transfer law

Under 11 U.S.C. § 544(b), the trustee inherits the longer lookback of any actual creditor's state-law fraudulent-transfer claim. Most states have enacted the Uniform Voidable Transactions Act (UVTA), which gives creditors 4 years from the transfer (or 1 year from discovery, whichever is later). A handful of states retain older Uniform Fraudulent Transfer Act timelines with similar effect.

Result: if you gave your adult child $30,000 three years before filing, the trustee may still be able to claw it back under state fraudulent-transfer law even though the federal § 548 2-year window has closed.

The Preference Elements (§ 547(b))

For the trustee to claw back a transfer, all five elements must be present:

  1. Transfer of the debtor's property. Cash payment, asset transfer, account funding.
  2. To or for the benefit of a creditor. The family member must be owed money (the loan they made to you).
  3. For or on account of an antecedent debt. The payment must be repayment of an earlier loan, not a gift or new-value exchange.
  4. Made while the debtor was insolvent. Insolvency is presumed in the 90 days before filing (§ 547(f)); for insiders in the extended 1-year window, the trustee must prove insolvency for the earlier period — which is rarely difficult if you filed at year-end.
  5. Gives the creditor more than they would get in a Chapter 7 liquidation. In a no-asset Chapter 7 (typical), unsecured creditors receive zero. Any dollar the family member received is "more than they would have gotten."

The defenses in § 547(c) — ordinary-course payments, contemporaneous exchange for new value, small-consumer exception (under $600 for consumer cases), domestic-support exception — rarely apply to family loans. Family loans are not "ordinary course," and the amounts are usually above the small-consumer floor.

What the Trustee Actually Does

Family-preference actions are routine. The standard sequence:

  1. Trustee reviews bank statements and the Statement of Financial Affairs. SOFA Question 4 (previously Question 3) asks specifically about payments to insiders in the year before filing. If you list the payment, the trustee sees it. If you do not list it, the trustee sees it anyway from bank records.
  2. Trustee sends a demand letter. Most trustees send a letter to the family member explaining the preference claim and demanding return of the funds. Many cases resolve at this stage — the family member pays back what they can afford, the trustee accepts a settlement.
  3. If no settlement, trustee files an adversary proceeding. This is a lawsuit inside the bankruptcy case (Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 7001). The family member must respond within 30 days, hire counsel, and litigate.
  4. Most adversaries settle. Trustees prefer cash in hand over protracted litigation. Typical settlements are 40-70% of the transferred amount, paid to the trustee over a few months.

The family member is not protected by your discharge. Your discharge wipes out your personal liability to creditors, but the trustee's claim against a third party who received a preference is independent. The family member has to defend the preference action themselves, pay their own counsel, and pay any judgment.

What NOT to Do

Do not try to "prefer" the family member by paying them off before filing. This is the exact scenario the clawback rule exists to catch. Paying your mom $5,000 the month before filing means your mom is the first call on the trustee's list.

Do not fail to list the debt on your schedules. Failing to list a family creditor is a false oath under 11 U.S.C. § 727(a)(4) — grounds for denial of discharge. List it on Schedule E/F and include the repayment on SOFA Question 4 if applicable.

Do not retroactively re-characterize the payment as a "gift." Some debtors try to label a recent repayment as a gift rather than a debt payment, thinking this avoids § 547. It does not — it shifts the claim from a preference (§ 547) to a fraudulent transfer (§ 548 or state law), often with a longer lookback. Worse, intentional mis-characterization is perjury.

Do not ask the family member to return the money to you before filing so you can "re-deposit" it. That creates a round-trip transaction the trustee sees on bank records and typically treats as evidence of actual intent to defraud — extending the lookback under § 548.

What To Do Instead

1. Stop repayment now. As of today, do not make any further payments on family loans if you are considering bankruptcy. Stop the payment stream. The sooner you stop, the sooner the clock starts running on the 1-year lookback.

2. Wait out the window if you can. If the last payment was 6 months ago and you can afford to wait another 6-12 months to file, the preference exposure disappears for the insider payment once the full 1-year window closes. (Non-insider 90-day window for other creditors still applies.)

3. Warn the family member and document the debt properly. If the loan was informal, document it now — promissory note with the original date, the amount, the repayment terms. This does not avoid the preference claim, but it avoids the secondary problem of the trustee treating "payment to mom" as a gift (fraudulent transfer, 2-4 year lookback) instead of loan repayment (preference, 1-year lookback).

4. Repay voluntarily after discharge if you want. Your post-discharge choices are yours. Many filers repay family out of post-bankruptcy income because the moral obligation survives. Post-discharge payments are not preferences — the estate is closed, the trustee has no claim.

5. Talk to a lawyer before the first consultation turns into a filing. Family-debt scenarios are the #1 reason "simple" Chapter 7 cases need attorney planning. A $200 consult on timing and disclosure saves family members from $5,000 preference settlements.

Common Scenarios

"I paid my mom back $3,000 six months ago"

Inside the 1-year insider window. The trustee sees the payment on your bank statements and in your SOFA disclosure. Expect a demand letter to your mom. Typical resolution: $1,200-$2,000 paid back to the trustee, settled within 4-6 months of filing. If you cannot file for another 6 months, wait — the exposure drops to zero once the 12-month window closes.

"My dad co-signed my car loan and I'm still making payments"

Different analysis. Monthly payments on a secured loan are not preferences if the value received equals the payment (ordinary-course defense). But if you made a large lump-sum payoff to protect your dad's co-signer liability, that payoff looks like a preference to the co-signer-as-creditor. Normal monthly payments: safe. Lump-sum payoffs: not safe.

"My sister gave me $10,000 two years ago — does that get clawed back?"

Opposite direction: gifts to you from family are not preferences (you are not paying them). But if you later repay that gift before filing, the repayment is a preference under the 1-year insider rule. The initial gift is only at risk if the giver files bankruptcy (their case, not yours).

"I transferred the title of my boat to my son last year"

Fraudulent transfer territory (§ 548 + state law). If the transfer was for less than reasonably equivalent value — i.e., you gave him the boat rather than sold it at market — the trustee can undo it. The 2-year federal window is closed only if more than 2 years have passed; the 4-year state UVTA window is still open.

Frequently Asked

Can my mom just refuse to give the money back?

She can refuse, but the trustee will sue her in an adversary proceeding. If she loses, the court enters a judgment against her for the preference amount plus costs. Most family members settle rather than litigate, because settlement costs less than counsel.

Does it matter if my mom knew I was insolvent?

No. Knowledge or good faith is not an element of the preference claim — the trustee only has to prove the five elements in § 547(b). Unlike fraudulent-transfer claims, preferences are strict-liability against the insider.

What if the family member already spent the money?

Not a defense. The claim is for the dollar value of the transfer, not for specific funds. If mom repaid a mortgage with the $3,000, the trustee still has a claim against mom for $3,000.

Will the family member's credit be affected?

No. Preference-defendant status does not appear on credit reports. A judgment may appear in public court records, but the adversary proceeding itself does not report to credit bureaus.

What if I filed pro se and didn't disclose the family payment?

Amend your Statement of Financial Affairs immediately to disclose. Failure to disclose, once discovered, is much worse than voluntary amendment. Voluntary corrections are routine and rarely draw consequences; forced corrections can trigger § 727(a)(4) discharge denial.
