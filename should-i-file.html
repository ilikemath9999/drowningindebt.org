Should I File Bankruptcy? A Three-Test Decision Framework

The answer is not a credit score, not a debt total. It is three specific tests — and the scenario you are actually in.

Free eligibility screener →

"Should I file bankruptcy" is the most-asked question among people carrying unpayable debt, and it is also the most over-answered. Debt relief companies say no (you become their customer). Credit counselors say maybe (you become their customer). Bankruptcy attorneys say yes (you become their customer). None of them are wrong in every case; all of them are wrong in some case.

The honest answer comes from three tests you can run on yourself in about ten minutes.

Test 1 — The Debt-to-Income Ratio

Add up your unsecured debt. Divide by your annual gross income.

Unsecured debt = credit cards, medical bills, personal loans, collection accounts, unpaid utility bills, old leases, deficiency balances after repossession. Do not count mortgages or car loans that you plan to keep paying.

Under 20%: bankruptcy is almost never the right answer. A debt management plan through a nonprofit credit counselor or direct payoff will resolve the situation without a ten-year credit-report entry.
20% to 40%: the gray zone. Depends on your age, job stability, and how fast interest is compounding. Run Test 2 next.
Over 40%: mathematically very hard to pay off without dramatic income increase or asset sale. Bankruptcy moves from option to likely-best option.
Over 100%: you owe more unsecured than you make in a year. Outside a lottery win, this does not resolve without bankruptcy or a settlement that itself creates tax liability.

Test 2 — The Time-to-Payoff

At your current monthly surplus, how long until zero?

Take your monthly income, subtract rent/mortgage, food, utilities, insurance, transportation, and minimum payments on everything you plan to keep. What is left is your surplus. Divide your total unsecured debt by that surplus.

Under 3 years: push through. The bankruptcy hit is not worth it.
3 to 5 years: borderline. A Chapter 13 plan is five years and discharges what is left — if that is what you are already doing voluntarily, you may as well get the discharge.
Over 5 years: your interest is probably outpacing your principal. You will still owe money in 2030 if nothing changes. Bankruptcy is the cheaper answer once you count the interest you would have paid.
Over 10 years or negative: the math does not work. Continuing to pay is continuing to lose.

Test 3 — Active Enforcement

Is anyone currently suing, garnishing, repossessing, or foreclosing?

This changes the calculus even if Tests 1 and 2 say "maybe." Once a creditor gets a judgment, they can garnish up to 25% of disposable wages in most states (less in some, like North Carolina, Pennsylvania, South Carolina, Texas). Once repossession starts, you have days, not weeks. Once foreclosure is filed, you are on a court calendar.

Filing triggers the automatic stay under 11 U.S.C. section 362. The moment the petition is filed — not accepted, not approved, filed — creditors must stop. Lawsuits pause. Garnishments stop with the next pay cycle. Repo trucks turn around. Foreclosure sales are canceled.
If you are under active enforcement, bankruptcy is no longer a financial question. It is a clock question.

If You Score Two "Yes" Answers

The rough decision rule: any two of the three tests pointing to bankruptcy means the conversation is no longer "should I" — it is "Chapter 7 or Chapter 13." The chapter comparison depends on your means-test result, your state exemptions, and whether you have secured debts you want to save (house, car).

Scenario Walkthroughs

"$13,000 in credit card debt, new $24/hour job, rent paid"

Test 1: $13k debt on ~$50k gross income = 26%. Gray zone.
Test 2: $500/mo surplus payoff = 26 months. Under 3 years.
Test 3: No lawsuits mentioned.
Read: Do not file. Call a nonprofit credit counselor, negotiate balance transfers or a debt management plan, and push through. Bankruptcy would solve the debt but would cost you credit access you will need as your income grows.

"$90,000 in credit card debt, single earner, $70,000 salary"

Test 1: $90k on $70k = 129%. Over 100%.
Test 2: $800/mo surplus ÷ $90k = 9.3 years at simple math — over 20 years once interest is modeled.
Test 3: Default is approaching; lawsuits likely within 12 months.
Read: File. The numbers do not resolve any other way. Chapter 7 if you pass the means test (state median for household size); Chapter 13 if you do not. Either way, the 10-year credit entry costs you less than paying 9+ years of interest that does not reduce your balance.

"Drowning in medical debt after uninsured hospitalization"

Test 1: Medical debt is almost always the cleanest case for Chapter 7 — fully dischargeable, no fraud allegations, sympathetic facts.
Test 2: Hospital bills often exceed annual income by multiples.
Test 3: Medical collection suits are routine.
Read: File. Medical-caused bankruptcy is the most common type in the United States — an estimated two-thirds of filings involve medical bills as a contributing factor. Chapter 7 is designed for this.

"I owe money to my mom / my parents / a friend"

Read with caution. If you repay a family loan within one year of filing — even a partial payment — the trustee can sue your family member to claw it back under the insider-preference rules (11 U.S.C. section 547(b)). One year is the reach-back window for insiders; 90 days for everyone else. If you are considering filing and you owe family, stop repayment now and talk to a lawyer about whether to list the debt, when to file, and what timing protects your family member. Do not try to "prefer" them by paying them off first — that creates the exact scenario the clawback rule is designed to catch.

"I am on a deed to my parents' house"

Read with caution. Being on a deed — even as a nominal or survivor interest — is a property interest under the Bankruptcy Code, and the trustee may be able to reach it. Equity you do not occupy or control is not protected by the homestead exemption in most states. Before filing, see whether you can be removed from the deed (not to hide the asset — you must disclose the prior ownership) or whether your state's exemption scheme protects non-occupant interests. This is not a DIY scenario.

When Bankruptcy Is Not the Answer

  • Almost all your debt is student loans. Student loans are only dischargeable on an undue-hardship showing (Brunner test or the 2022 DOJ Attorney's Guide). Bankruptcy may still help discharge other debt so you can afford loan payments — but it will not erase the loans on its own.
  • You transferred assets recently. Anything you gave away, sold at below-market, or moved to a trust or family member in the prior two to four years (state-dependent lookback) can be clawed back as a fraudulent transfer. If you made large transfers, time matters — talk to a lawyer before filing.
  • You owe primarily domestic support or recent taxes. Child support, alimony, and taxes under three years old do not discharge. Bankruptcy will stop enforcement temporarily but the debt survives.
  • You have pending fraud or intentional-tort claims. Debts for fraud, willful injury, or embezzlement are nondischargeable under 11 U.S.C. section 523(a).

Frequently Asked

How much debt is enough to file bankruptcy?

No legal minimum. Practical floor: $10,000 is usually the point where the cost of filing ($300–$2,000 depending on chapter and attorney) plus the credit-report hit is outweighed by the discharge. Under $10k, try alternatives first.

Will I lose my house or my car?

Usually no. Chapter 13 is specifically designed to save houses and cars. Chapter 7 protects them up to your state's homestead and vehicle exemption amounts — if you are current on payments and have limited equity, you keep them. State-by-state exemption amounts.

How long does the credit hit last?

Chapter 7: 10 years on your credit report. Chapter 13: 7 years. But the practical impact drops sharply after 2 years — mortgages are available at 2–4 years post-discharge, car loans at 1–2 years, credit cards at 6–12 months.

Can I file bankruptcy twice?

Yes, subject to time bars. The re-filing rules under 11 U.S.C. section 1328(f) and section 727(a)(8) set minimum gaps between discharges — 8 years between two Chapter 7s, 4 years from Ch 7 to Ch 13, 2 years between two Chapter 13s. Run your situation through the screener if you have filed before.

What if I have a co-signer?

Your discharge wipes your liability. The co-signer is still on the hook unless you file Chapter 13, which extends the co-debtor stay to them for the life of the plan. If protecting a co-signer matters, Chapter 13 is often the right chapter even when Chapter 7 would qualify.
