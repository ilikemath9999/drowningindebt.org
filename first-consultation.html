Your First Bankruptcy Consultation - What to Bring, What to Expect

The free consultation is an assessment, not a sales pitch. Come prepared, ask the right questions, and leave without hiring if the fit is wrong.

Run the screener first →

Most consumer bankruptcy attorneys offer a free 30- to 60-minute initial consultation. That window exists for both sides - you decide whether to hire the attorney; the attorney decides whether to take the case. Treating it as anything other than a two-way assessment leads to poor fits: overpriced flat fees, case-mill shops where your lawyer's name is on the filing but a paralegal handles every interaction, or outright case-neglect.

This page is about making that one hour count.

What to Bring

Documents (bring copies; leave originals home)

  • List of all debts - creditor name, account number (last four is fine), current balance, monthly payment. Credit report pull is the easiest way to assemble this.
  • Last two months of pay stubs - for every job, every household earner who will be on the filing.
  • Last two years of tax returns - full returns with schedules, not just the 1040.
  • Bank statements, last 60 days - every account you own or have signature authority on.
  • Property list with rough values - home estimate from Zillow/Redfin, car year/make/model/mileage, retirement account balances, anything you own worth more than $500.
  • Lawsuits, garnishment notices, collection letters - anything with a deadline or court date.
  • Prior bankruptcy paperwork - if you have filed before, bring the case number and discharge order (or dismissal order).
  • Government photo ID and Social Security card - the attorney may not copy them at the consult, but will need them eventually.

Mental checklist (no documents required)

  • A rough timeline of when the financial trouble started and what caused it (job loss, medical event, divorce, business failure).
  • Large recent financial moves - sold a car, cashed out retirement, paid a family member back, gave someone a large gift, moved money between accounts. The attorney needs to know about these; some create clawback risk.
  • Whether anyone else is depending on this decision - co-signed debts, spouse's filing decision, family financial entanglements.
  • What outcome you want - debt gone as fast as possible (Chapter 7 lens), keep the house and car at all costs (Chapter 13 lens), or you genuinely do not know.

What the Attorney Will Ask

Expect the attorney to drive the first half of the meeting. The standard intake flow:

  1. Summary of your situation. Two-minute overview: household income, total debt, what is driving the crisis, what you have tried.
  2. Means test pre-screen. Gross household income versus state median for your household size. Determines whether Chapter 7 is available or Chapter 13 is required. State-specific medians are published on the U.S. Trustee Program's website.
  3. Asset and exemption triage. Do you have a house? Equity? Car with a loan? Retirement? The attorney is checking whether your state exemptions cover what you own.
  4. Recent-moves check. Any asset sales, family-loan payoffs, property transfers, or large withdrawals in the last 1-4 years. Trustees can claw back transfers under 11 U.S.C. § 547 (preferences, 90 days or 1 year for insiders) and § 548 (fraudulent transfers, 2 years).
  5. Prior filings. Any previous bankruptcy in the last 8 years - triggers the 1328(f) filing bar analysis.
  6. Lawsuits and garnishments. Active enforcement changes the timing of a filing.

By the end of that intake, the attorney should be able to tell you (a) Chapter 7 or 13, (b) approximate timeline, (c) flat fee, and (d) what documents to bring back if you decide to move forward.

What You Should Ask

The best consultations end with you asking five questions. Write them down. An attorney who will not give clear answers to these is not the attorney to hire.

1. "Chapter 7 or Chapter 13 - which do you recommend for my situation, and why?"

You want a specific reason tied to your facts, not a rote "Chapter 7 is cheaper." An attorney who will not commit to a chapter at the end of a free consult either needs more information (legitimate - ask what) or is avoiding a firm answer (not legitimate).

2. "What is your flat fee and what does it include?"

Fee ranges vary by district, but typical ranges in 2026: Chapter 7 $1,200-$2,500 flat plus $338 filing fee; Chapter 13 $3,500-$5,000 with most of the attorney fee paid through the plan. Ask exactly what is included (341 meeting, schedules, initial plan) and what costs extra (amendments, reaffirmations, motions, adversary proceedings, conversions).

3. "Will you personally handle my case, or will it be assigned to an associate?"

High-volume shops often assign most work to paralegals and junior associates. That is not automatically bad - but you should know before you sign. If the answer is "the paralegal will handle routine work but I will personally appear at your 341 and any hearings," that is different from "I oversee every case" (which often means the opposite in practice).

4. "How many active cases do you currently have?"

A solo bankruptcy attorney with 150+ active cases is almost certainly running a high-volume practice with minimal individual attention. Firms with 3-5 attorneys can handle higher caseloads cleanly. Ask, note the answer, and weight the rest of the consult against it.

5. "Have you been disciplined by the state bar? Do you have pending complaints?"

Most attorneys will answer honestly because the answers are public record and lying at a consult is itself misconduct. State bar discipline records are searchable online in every state - verify the answer before you sign. Past discipline does not automatically disqualify; pattern of complaints does.

Red Flags in the Consultation

The attorney never actually meets with you. Paralegal handles intake, another paralegal quotes the fee, you sign a fee agreement without ever speaking to an attorney. If that is the consultation experience, the representation experience will be the same. Walk out.

Pressure to sign the fee agreement today. "We have a slot this week" or "the fee goes up after Friday" tactics exist in bankruptcy practice, almost always at high-volume shops. A legitimate attorney will give you two or three days to decide.

Promises that are not theirs to make. "I guarantee you will keep your house." "You will definitely qualify for Chapter 7." No bankruptcy attorney can guarantee outcomes - they depend on the trustee, the U.S. Trustee's office, and creditor objections. Specific guarantees mean the attorney is selling, not assessing.

Unwillingness to discuss alternatives. If your situation is borderline (Chapter 7 vs. debt management plan vs. direct payoff), the honest attorney will lay out the alternatives and explain why their recommendation fits. An attorney who refuses to discuss non-bankruptcy options is selling one product.

"Just sign everything now and we will figure out the details." Bankruptcy filings are sworn documents. Signing incomplete schedules or blank forms creates personal liability and can be grounds for denial of discharge under 11 U.S.C. § 727(a)(4). Never sign schedules you have not read.

Comparing Two or Three Attorneys

Run the same consultation with two or three attorneys before deciding. The cost is 2-3 hours of your time; the payoff is a significantly better fit and, often, $500-$1,500 in fee savings.

Write down after each consultation:

  • Chapter recommendation + reason
  • Flat fee + what is included
  • Time estimate from hire to discharge
  • How many active cases
  • Who handles what (attorney vs. paralegal)
  • Your gut read on the fit

If one attorney is a clear outlier on any dimension (fee twice the others, case load triple the others, chapter recommendation the others rejected), treat that as a signal to dig deeper - not automatically a disqualification.

When to Leave Without Hiring

A free consultation imposes zero obligation. You can leave at any point - including mid-meeting. "This is not the right fit for me" is a complete sentence. Attorneys who respect their profession accept it cleanly. Attorneys who get hostile at that sentence were going to be difficult to work with anyway.

Legitimate reasons to leave without hiring:

  • The fee is higher than you can afford, and payment plans are not offered.
  • The attorney cannot clearly explain your chapter options.
  • You do not speak with the attorney directly during the consult.
  • The attorney dismisses or rushes your questions.
  • Your gut says no.

If finances are the barrier, ask about: flat-fee Chapter 7 with payment plan, sliding-scale community legal aid, bankruptcy law school clinics (UMKC, Mizzou, SLU in Missouri; UMKC Law's Bankruptcy Clinic is one example nationally), and LawHelp.org state-by-state legal aid finders.

Frequently Asked

Can I consult by phone or video instead of in person?

Yes. Video and phone consultations are standard since 2020. Document review works fine over email or a secure upload portal. In-person consults are not required.

Do I have to tell the truth at the consultation?

The consultation is not under oath, but the petition you later sign is. Hiding assets or debts from your attorney means the attorney cannot protect them (or you). Attorney-client privilege covers what you disclose; the filing does not.

What if I consult and then decide not to file?

That is exactly what the consultation is for. No obligation, no fee (for a free consult), no filing. The attorney can keep your intake file or destroy it at your request.

Can my spouse come with me?

Yes, and often should - especially in community property states or if you are considering a joint filing. Spousal communications retain their privilege.

What if I am filing pro se?

You can still do a paid consult (flat-fee unbundled services) with a bankruptcy attorney for advice on specific issues (schedules, 341 prep, motions). Pro se filers in complex cases often benefit from a 1-2 hour paid consult before filing, even if not retaining full representation. See pro se bankruptcy for the procedural baseline.
